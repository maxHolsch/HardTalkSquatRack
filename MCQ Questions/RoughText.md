Conversation Skills Assessment: Workplace Edition

SKILL 1: Distinguishing Facts from Interpretations
This skill involves separating what actually happened (observable behaviors, specific words, concrete events) from the meaning, motive, or story we assign to those behaviors.

Type A: Which response best demonstrates separating what actually happened from the meaning assigned to it?

Q1. Your manager scheduled a meeting with you but didn't say what it was about. Which response best separates observable facts from interpretation?
A) "She scheduled a 30-minute meeting for tomorrow afternoon without specifying the topic."
B) "She's probably going to give me negative feedback—she always does this before bad news."
C) "She's testing me to see how I react to uncertainty."
D) "She must have something urgent and confidential to discuss."
Answer: A
Rationale: A states only what observably happened. B assumes negative intent based on a pattern that may not exist. C attributes a psychological motive. D assumes urgency and confidentiality without evidence.
Difficulty: Easy

Q2. During a video call, your colleague turned off their camera after you started presenting your quarterly results. Which response best demonstrates identifying what actually occurred versus the story you might tell about it?
A) "They turned off their camera while I was presenting—I'm not sure why."
B) "They're clearly bored by my presentation."
C) "They're probably multitasking because they don't prioritize this meeting."
D) "They're being passive-aggressive about my promotion last month."
Answer: A
Rationale: A describes the behavior and acknowledges uncertainty. B, C, and D all assign motives without evidence—boredom, deprioritization, and passive-aggression respectively.
Difficulty: Easy

Q3. A coworker CC'd your manager on an email asking you to clarify a data discrepancy. Which thought best reflects separating what happened from the interpretation?
A) "They're trying to get me in trouble by involving my manager."
B) "They CC'd my manager on a question about the data discrepancy in the Q3 report."
C) "They don't trust me to handle this correctly."
D) "They want my manager to see that I made a mistake."
Answer: B
Rationale: B describes the observable action. A, C, and D all assume negative intent—getting in trouble, distrust, and exposing mistakes.
Difficulty: Easy

Q4. Your team lead gave you detailed notes on your project proposal, with more comments than anyone else on the team received. Which internal response best demonstrates distinguishing the observable event from the story you might create about it?

B) "They're micromanaging me because they don't respect my experience."
C) "They're investing extra time in me because I'm being groomed for leadership."
D) "I received 14 comments on my proposal; I don't know their reasoning without asking."

Rationale: D states the fact and acknowledges the limits of knowledge. A assumes substandard work. B assumes disrespect. C assumes positive favoritism. All three interpretations could be wrong.
Difficulty: Medium

Q5. In a performance review, your manager said "Your attention to detail has room for improvement." You're preparing to discuss this further. Which framing best separates observation from interpretation?

SKILL 2: Reflective Listening (Paraphrasing + Emotion Acknowledgment)
This skill involves restating what someone said in a way that shows you accurately understood their message AND recognized their emotional experience. It increases psychological safety and reduces defensiveness.

Type A: Which response best demonstrates restating someone's message while acknowledging their emotional experience?

Q26. Your direct report says: "I've been handling three people's workloads since the layoffs, and I just found out I'm not getting a raise this year."
Which response best demonstrates restating their message while showing you understand how they feel?
A) "The budget for raises is frozen across the whole department, so it's not personal."
B) "Have you talked to HR about a title change instead?"
C) "You've been carrying a huge load since the restructure, and now learning there's no raise—that must feel like a real slap in the face."
D) "I understand. Lots of people are stretched thin right now."
Answer: C
Rationale: C paraphrases the situation (heavy load, no raise) and names the likely emotional impact. A deflects with context. B jumps to solutions. D minimizes by comparing to others.
Difficulty: Easy

Q27. A colleague says: "I put in 60-hour weeks to land the Morrison account, and then they gave the account lead position to someone who wasn't even on the pitch team."
Which response best shows you've heard both what happened and how it landed for them?
A) "Maybe there are factors you're not aware of in the decision."
B) "You should document your contributions and raise this with your manager."
C) "You put in an enormous effort to win this account, and then someone else got the lead role. That sounds incredibly frustrating and unfair."
D) "Account assignments are always political. Don't take it personally."
Answer: C
Rationale: C reflects the content (effort, outcome) and emotional experience (frustrating, unfair). A introduces doubt. B jumps to action. D dismisses the emotional impact.
Difficulty: Easy

Difficulty: Easy
